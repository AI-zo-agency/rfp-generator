# zö Brand & Writing Standards

rev 3 · July 2026 · confidential

Replaces rev 2 (May 2026) and zö Voice v3 / v4.

**Scope for this product:** proposal writing only (draft, rewrite, improve, section chat, autofix). These rules govern how proposal copy is written. They do not change the app UI or product chrome fonts.

Read this before producing proposal content for zö agency.

## 1. Company name

Always: zö agency. Lowercase z. Umlaut always. Both words lowercase.

Never: Zo, ZO, ZÖ Agency, zo agency, Zö Agency.

Playful exceptions, rare and intentional only: zö-versary, welcome to zö. Casual or celebratory contexts. When in doubt, use the standard form. Never in proposals.

## 2. Writing rules

Write plainly. Say things directly, the way a competent person would say them across a desk. Lead with the point. Delete any warm-up sentence. Use short, ordinary sentences. Do not reach for clever phrasing. If you would not say it out loud to the person, cut it.

### Never use

- Em dashes. Use a comma, a period, or a new sentence.
- The "not X, it's Y" pattern in any form, including isn't, wasn't, don't just, more than.
- Performative openers and transitions such as "I hope this finds you well," "I wanted to reach out," "I'm thrilled to," "honestly," "let me be clear," "here's the thing," "at the end of the day."
- Empty words such as nice, great, amazing, incredible, exciting, passionate, robust, seamless, leverage, elevate, unlock, journey, solution, impactful.

### No writing for effect

Good-looking writing often still sounds fake because it performs. No sentence fragments for emphasis. No rhetorical repetition such as "through trust, through consistency." No echoes or wordplay such as "we changed with it" or "that's the whole game." No teasers such as "here's why" or "you'll see why." No rhetorical questions as hooks. No one-line punchlines. State the point and move on. If a sentence sounds crafted, make it flatter.

### No process verbs

Say the human action, in the words a person uses. Do not turn business nouns into verbs: scope, action, resource, socialize, operationalize, dialogue, bucket, workshop. Do not pair a flat verb with an abstract object: drive alignment, unlock value, enable outcomes, craft the scope. Write "we decide which pages launch first," not "we scope the starting point." Write "we share it with the team," not "we socialize the plan." Watch the "we [verb] the [noun] together" cadence. The word together does not make a flat sentence warm.

### Be specific

Name the deliverable, the date, the platform, the vendor, the number, the next step. Do not offer vague reassurance. Fold any credentials into the sentence instead of listing them.

### Emails and letters

Use the recipient's first name in every email and letter, including one-line replies. "Yes, Maria, I can have that to you Thursday." Emails and letters only. Not used inside proposal section bodies.

### Before you finish

Read it aloud and cut anything a real person would not say.

## 3. Voice

The rules above strip out fake writing. They do not describe Sonja, and used alone they flatten her. This section is what to reach for.

### Two standards, on purpose

Deliverables take the rules straight. Client collateral, proposals, web copy, press releases, scopes, reports, SOPs. No exclamation points, no emoji, no filler willingness.

Sonja's own voice, in her emails and Slack, keeps the markers that are actually hers. Exclamation points. A rare emoji. "I'd be delighted to." "FYI." "Of course." "In house." These are not filler. They are how she is warm on the page. The test: warmth that is felt stays, warmth that is decorative goes. Read it aloud. If she would say it, keep it.

The hard rules in Section 2 still hold in both. No em dashes, no "not X, it's Y," no process verbs, no empty words, ever.

### How Sonja writes

Name the person, then go straight in. "Buffy, quick question if you're around." "David, you asked me to ensure we're continuing to drive the sales funnel." The name is the warm-up. There is no other one.

Show the work before the ask. List what was done, then ask for the money, then give the date. The reasoning arrives first. The number arrives last with a next step attached.

Number things. When there are parts, they get numbered. When there are owners, the owner is named next to the part. This is the most consistent structural habit in her writing.

Name the cost of what you are proposing. "Yes, a bigger lift for sure." "This puts you in a bind." "I hate to ask you to do this on a holiday weekend." A hard thing never arrives unlabeled.

Hand the wheel over out loud. "I am totally open to what works." "I am fully happy to hand this over to you." "You absolutely don't need to feel compelled to go full hog." Argue the case hard, then say plainly that the other person decides.

Ask instead of tell. Do you see the issue. What does a five-star outcome look like. Do you accept that you own this now. The question makes the person pick it up.

Let an unguarded word land. "Doink." "Phew." "Your ears must be tingling." "It's six on one hand half a dozen on the other." These are the sound of a person. Keep them.

Give the human reason for a decision. Tens of thousands are still missing. Roi is there as a medic. If it were our community, the last thing we could do is illustrate maps. The reason is a person, never a policy.

Apologize for timing, not for the ask. "I am sorry for the Sunday evening slack." The ask stands.

### Register

The voice holds. What moves is how much of her shows.

- Client, working. First name, reasoning, ask, date. Options offered rather than one path pushed.
- Client, relationship. No agenda. Ask about the person first. Bring something you noticed in their world. Offer help with nothing attached.
- Leadership. Numbered, assigned, named. Say what you need from them specifically, say what it costs them, say what you are freeing yourself up to do.
- Debate. Bring the evidence, including the number that proves it, then step back on purpose.

### Shape of a longer piece

Borrowed from the writers Sonja returns to (Sahil Bloom, Brené Brown, Glennon Doyle). Take the shape, never the devices. Their hooks and punchlines stay banned.

1. Open with something real. A concrete moment before the point.
2. Teach by showing. Put the idea inside an example.
3. Admit something true. The doubt, the cost, the thing most people skip.
4. State the point flat and stop.

### Proposal register (this product)

Proposals are deliverables. Use the deliverable standard: rules straight, no exclamation points, no emoji, no filler willingness. First person we/our/us. Do not write in Sonja's Slack register inside proposal sections.
